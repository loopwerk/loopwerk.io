# Apple blames the DMA again

Apple will do almost anything to protect their walled garden, and opening it up for competitors is the one thing they refuse to do, even when that's exactly what Europe's Digital Markets Act requires. The DMA is a law aimed at "gatekeepers", the handful of companies so large that they effectively control how the rest of us access digital services. The whole point is to stop those gatekeepers from using their position to lock users in and shut competitors out. If Apple builds a feature, the law says it can't be built in a way that only ever works inside Apple's own ecosystem. That's not an attack on Apple. That's the entire reason the law exists.

You could clearly see how Apple feels about this with the iPhone mirroring feature, which we still don't have over here in Europe. The reason is that the DMA says Apple can't build a feature that locks in users. In other words, iPhone mirroring should also work on Windows or Linux. Which makes sense, right? If I were a Linux user and saw the mirroring feature announced at WWDC, I'd think "great, yet another thing that only works on macOS." That's lock-in, and that lock-in is the point: it's the thing that keeps you buying Apple hardware.

Apple could open up the mirroring feature. They just won't, because an open feature doesn't trap anyone. So instead nobody in Europe gets it, an entire continent of paying customers is being held hostage, and Apple blames it on the "extreme" DMA.

And iPhone mirroring isn't a one-off either. Do you remember Live Translation with AirPods, or Preferred Routes in Maps?

Every time, Apple says it hasn't found a way to share the feature with other developers without putting your data at risk. Maybe that's even true. But Apple, sitting on three trillion dollars and the best engineering talent on the planet, wants you to believe these problems are unsolvable? Somehow the answer is always "either I keep it locked to my hardware, or nobody gets it at all." These problems are not unsolvable. They're just inconvenient to Apple's business model.

Apple has been remarkably open about how it sees the situation. In a [September 2025 newsroom post](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2025/09/the-digital-markets-acts-impacts-on-eu-users/) titled "The Digital Markets Act's impacts on EU users", the company writes:

> The DMA requires Apple to make certain features work on non-Apple products and apps before we can share them with our users. If we shared them any sooner, we'd be fined and potentially forced to stop shipping our products in the EU. [...] The DMA also lets other companies request access to user data and core technologies of Apple products. Apple is required to meet almost every request, even if they create serious risks for our users.

The post wraps up with the line they clearly want you to walk away with: "It's becoming clear that the DMA is leading to a worse experience for Apple users in the EU." It's a smooth bit of PR. The EU made us do it! Brussels is why you can't have nice things! Be angry at the regulator, not at the company that decided withholding features was preferable to sharing them. It's a deflection, and a cynical one, because it counts on you not reading past the headline.

## Siri AI in 2026

They are using the same playbook now in 2026 with [Siri AI on iOS](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2026/06/due-to-dma-siri-ai-delayed-in-eu-for-ios-27-and-ipados-27/). The DMA says people can't be locked in to Apple's models, users need to be able to choose. Apple doesn't want other AI apps to have unfettered access to all user data, because of user privacy. Which is fair! According to Apple, the DMA would require them to "give any AI system nearly unlimited access to a user's device, as well as the ability to act on that access autonomously without a user's ongoing visibility and control." That includes "the ability to read and send messages, make purchases, access files, and execute actions across any app." Nobody wants that.

So they built a solution called Trusted System Agent, an intermediary that handles the data access and gives third-party AI systems the same kind of access that Siri has, without handing over the keys to the whole device. So far, so reasonable.

But then comes the part that gives the game away. Apple "shared a plan to launch Siri AI in the EU while gradually rolling out this new solution over an 18-month period". The European Commission said no to that, and so Apple cries foul:

> Apple will continue working to bring these features to the European Union as safely as possible. However, given the clear dangers to EU users and the regulators’ failure to acknowledge these risks, there is currently no timeline for Siri AI’s availability in the EU on iOS and iPadOS.

Read that 18-month request again, though. Apple is asking the EU for permission to ship a feature that doesn't comply with the law, and to keep shipping it that way for a year and a half, on the promise that the compliant version will arrive eventually. That's not the regulator being unreasonable. That's a company asking to break the law for 18 months and expecting everyone to just trust that the fix is coming, from the same company that has spent years finding reasons not to share iPhone mirroring, Maps history, or AirPods translation. Forgive me if I don't take that promise at face value.

## Apple chose this

The thing all of these stories have in common is that Apple always has another option, and it's always the same option: open the feature up the way the law asks. Every time, Apple decides that's worse than shipping nothing, and then dresses up "nothing" as something the cruel EU did to you.

And to be clear, the privacy concerns aren't fake. Giving arbitrary apps deep access to your device is genuinely dangerous, and "let users pick their assistant" can absolutely be done in a sloppy, unsafe way. But "privacy and security" is also Apple's favorite get-out-of-jail-free card, and they play it constantly. There is a difference between protecting your data and using that protection as a proprietary moat, and Apple has gotten very good at blurring the two. When they tell you the engineering is impossible, what they really mean is that they designed the architecture to be a dead end, on purpose, because a dead end keeps competitors out.

Because here's the thing: these are solvable problems, and Apple knows how to solve them. In fact, they already built the solution and gave it a snazzy name! But boo-hoo, poor Apple isn't allowed to break the law for 18 months, so they write a 1000 word anti-DMA article on their website instead.

So the next time you see Apple blame the DMA for a missing feature, finish the sentence for them. The DMA didn't take the feature away. Apple did, because the alternative was giving you a choice, and a real choice is the one thing the walled garden was never designed to allow.
